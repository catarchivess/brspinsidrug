# Use of Network Pharmacology and Molecular Docking to Explore the Mechanism of Action of Curcuma in the Treatment of Osteosarcoma

# Background
Osteosarcoma is a malignant bone tumors originating from bone tissue, most appearing in the distal femur and proximal tibia. The standard treatment for Osteosarcoma includes surgical resection combined with preoperative and postoperative adjuvant radiotherapies, yet despite its effective result, the recurrence of Osteosarcoma remains high. Traditional Chinese Method (TCM) battled this using Curcuma, a dried rhizome of the ginger plant curcuma, usually used to treat chest pain and other diseases. It is shown that noncurcumin compounds in curcuma have anticancer activity. Yet, its complex molecular mechanisms of active antitumor are poorly explored

This study aims to determine the therapeutic targets and potential signalling pathways most closely related to Osteosarcoma to determine the potential mechanisms or curcuma as an anticancer agent in this disease, using network pharmacology and molecular docking.

The study started by obtaining all curcuma compounds with anticancer activity from relevant literatures, curcuma-related targets with a probability greater than 0 were predicted using the Swiss Target Prediction database. Then the Osteosarcoma associated targets were searched. The STRING database was used to analyze the relationship of common curcuma and Osteosarcoma related targets, which were considered potential therapeutic targets. The network of active compound signalling pathways was visualised using Cytoscape v3.8 software. A molecular docking approach was applied to analyze the relationship between the key targets of curcuma and the core compound. Finally, the reliability of the docking results was assessed by calculating the root mean square deviation value using PyMOL software.

The study identified 11 active anticancer compounds from curcuma with favorable pharmacokinetic properties and 141 common therapeutic targets associated with osteosarcoma. Protein-protein interaction analysis identified 12 hub genes, with AKT1, TNF, STAT3, EGFR, and HSP90AA1 as the most significant targets. GO and KEGG enrichment analyses revealed that the therapeutic effects of curcuma were mainly associated with the PI3K/Akt, HIF-1, FOXO, ErbB, and chemokine signalling pathways, which regulate angiogenesis, cell proliferation, metastasis, invasion, and chemotherapy resistance. Molecular docking further demonstrated that curcumin exhibited strong binding affinity toward AKT1, TNF, STAT3, and EGFR, with binding energies below −5 kJ/mol and RMSD values below 2 Å, indicating stable and reliable interactions between the ligand and target proteins. 

The study successfully integrated network pharmacology with molecular docking to comprehensively investigate the multi-component and multi-target therapeutic mechanisms of curcuma against osteosarcoma. The use of several public databases improved target coverage and strengthened the reliability of the computational predictions. However, the findings were entirely based on bioinformatics analysis and molecular docking without experimental validation. In addition, factors such as osteosarcoma stage, dosage variation, and the complex biological environment were not considered, which may influence the therapeutic effects of curcuma in clinical settings.
This study highlights that curcuma exerts its anticancer activity through a multi-target and multi-pathway mechanism rather than acting on a single molecular target. Curcumin was identified as the major bioactive compound capable of regulating several key proteins involved in osteosarcoma progression, suggesting its potential role in reducing tumour growth, metastasis, and chemotherapy resistance. The study also demonstrates how network pharmacology combined with molecular docking provides an efficient strategy to predict the molecular mechanisms of traditional Chinese medicine and identify promising therapeutic targets before conducting expensive in vitro and in vivo experiments.
